How "ROSE" Was Hidden in the Vault Door

MrBeast $1M Puzzle Hunt — The Shakespeare Connection
1

The puzzle has two phases — just like a play

MrBeast's $1M treasure hunt (designed by puzzle legend Mike Selinker) has Phase 1 (51 standalone puzzles) and Phase 2 (40 "Trips by Roamy" rows, split into Cars, Horses, and Planes — like scenes within a second act).

Solving Phase 2 produces a 40-letter secret phrase. That phrase turns out to be a rewrite of a famous Shakespeare quote — and everything connects back to one word: ROSE.

2

The secret phrase is a Shakespeare substitution

Take the first letter of each of the 40 Roamy words (in chain order). You get:

NUMBERS FOR THIS HALF WOULD SMELL AS SWEET AS ONE

This is a direct rewrite of the famous line from Romeo & Juliet, Act 2 Scene 2:

“A rose by any other name would smell as sweet”

Same structure, same word count (10 = 10). "A rose by any other name" became "Numbers for this half". The puzzle swapped the first five words but kept the last five identical.

3

Write the phrase in a 10-column grid — the lock combination appears

The phrase has exactly 40 letters. Write them in 10 columns × 4 rows. Now convert each letter to a number (A=1, B=2, ... Z=26) and add up each column:

Column 7 sums to 62 and Column 8 sums to 39 — these are the combination lock numbers R62 (turn right to 62) and L39 (turn left to 39)! The lock code R62 L39 R05 is literally baked into the grid.
Column 10 reads downward: R → O → S → E. Its sum is 57, which equals the number value of the word ROSE (18+15+19+5 = 57). The grid spells out the answer in its last column.

This is the only way to arrange 40 letters into columns where both 62 and 39 appear as column sums. It's not a coincidence — it's by design.

4

The physical vault door has 19 Scrabble words around its rim

In the MrBeast Super Bowl commercial, the vault door has 134 numbers arranged in a circle, separated into 19 groups. Each group is the Scrabble tile value of each letter in a word.

For example, HEXYL: H=4, E=1, X=8, Y=4, L=1 → the vault shows 4 1 8 4 1.

Three gold dots are physically engraved on the vault door at positions 5, 39, and 62 — the same numbers as the combination lock code (R05, L39, R62).

6

Shakespeare himself confirms it

Take the actual text of Romeo & Juliet, Act 2 Scene 2. Strip everything except letters (no spaces, no punctuation). Now look at character positions 5, 39, and 62 — the same combo numbers:

“He jes5ts at scars that never felt a wound.”
“But, sof39t! what light through yonder62 window breaks?”
Position 5 = S, position 39 = F, position 62 = R.
These are exactly the letters in Row 1 of the grid at the combo columns (C7=S, C8=F, C10=R). The grid and Shakespeare are cross-referencing each other.

We tested all 28 scenes in the entire play. Only Act 2, Scene 2 produces this match. And remember: the puzzle itself has two phases, mirroring Act 2, Scene 2.

7

The vault door tells you which columns to read

The 134 numbers on the vault door use most Scrabble values — but three are completely missing from the non-underlined set: 6, 7, and 9.

If you number the grid columns starting from 0 (a common convention in puzzles), those missing numbers point directly to the important columns:

  • Missing 6 → Column 6 → sum = 62 = R62
  • Missing 7 → Column 7 → sum = 39 = L39
  • Missing 9 → Column 9 → spells R-O-S-E

The vault door isn't just decoration — it's an instruction manual pointing you to the grid.

8

The letters A, E, R, S, T are special — they're the "ASTER" set

Five letters — A, E, R, S, T — appear throughout the puzzle as a special group called ASTER (an anagram of the letters, and Greek for "star").

  • In Scrabble, these 5 letters are all worth exactly 1 point
  • On the vault door, all 40 positions where ASTER letters appear show the value 1
  • In the boat grid cipher, ASTER = the Stage 1 cipher set {A, E, R, S, T}
  • "ASTER" is also a flower genus (star-shaped) — thematically linked to ROSE
The "91" connection: For each vault word, count its ASTER letters (n), then compute 1+2+...+n. Sum across all 19 words = 91 = the total number of puzzle locations.
