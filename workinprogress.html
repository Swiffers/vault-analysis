10×4 Shakespeare Grid

The 40-letter Roamy phrase "NUMBERS FOR THIS HALF WOULD SMELL AS SWEET AS ONE" is a Shakespeare substitution — it replaces "A rose by any other name would smell as sweet" from Romeo & Juliet, Act 2 Scene 2.

Write it in 10 columns × 4 rows. Convert each letter to its number (A=1, Z=26) and add up each column. Two column sums jump out: Col 7 = 62 and Col 8 = 39 — the combination lock numbers R62 and L39. Column 10 reads downward as R-O-S-E, and its sum (57) equals A1Z26(ROSE). This is the only grid arrangement of the phrase where both 62 and 39 appear as column sums.

The vault door circle has Scrabble tile values for 19 words — but three values are missing from the non-underlined set: {6, 7, 9}. Using 0-indexed columns (C0–C9), those exact numbers point to the combo columns: 6→Col 6 (sum 62 = R62), 7→Col 7 (sum 39 = L39), 9→Col 9 (spells ROSE). How ROSE Emerges from the Vault Door

The vault door has 19 Scrabble words totaling 134 letters arranged in a circle. 18 separator dots divide the 19 word groups (shown as grey lines on the bar below). Three of these positions are gold combo dots at positions 5, 39, and 62 — the combination lock numbers (R05, L39, R62).

Positions 5 and 39 land exactly on group boundaries (after HEXYL and ATAXY). Position 62 falls inside word #11 CONFLUX (which spans 62–68), not on a boundary. Add up each word's Scrabble tile values: 18, 15, 19. Convert those totals to letters (A=1 … Z=26): 18→R, 15→O, 19→S. The combo's first number R05 = 5 → E. Together: R + O + S + E = ROSE — the Shakespeare keyword.

Bonus: the actual characters sitting at dot positions 5, 39, 62 are L, Y, C. Their A1Z26 values sum to 12+25+3 = 40 = the number of Roamy rows.

Combo Numbers = ROSE Decomposed

The three dot-word Scrabble sums (18 + 15 + 19 = 52) plus R05 (5) = 57 = A1Z26(ROSE). But it goes deeper — every combo number is a piece of ROSE:

Combo Formula Value
R05 A1Z26(E) — last letter of ROSE 5
L39 A1Z26(O+S+E) — ROSE minus R 15 + 19 + 5 = 39
R62 A1Z26(ROSE) + R05 — full word + E 57 + 5 = 62
Self-referential identity: A1Z26(R) + L39 + R05 = 18 + 39 + 5 = 62 = R62.
The combo numbers aren't arbitrary — they're nested decompositions of the word ROSE.

Combo Columns = ROSE ± Its Own Letters

In the 10×4 grid, three columns have sums that equal the combination lock numbers. Each of those sums can be written as ROSE (57) plus or minus a letter of ROSE itself:

Column Letters ↓ Sum = ROSE … Combo
C8  [7] F · F · L · O 39 ROSE(57) − R(18) = L39
C10 [9] R · O · S · E 57 ROSE itself = ROSE
C7  [6] S · L · L · S 62 ROSE(57) + E(5) = R62
And R05 = E = 5 — just the last letter of ROSE on its own.

In plain English: ROSE is the center of the lock. To get each combo number, you only need the first letter (R) and last letter (E) of ROSE as operators: subtract R to go left (L39), add E to go right (R62), or take E alone (R05). The middle letters O and S never move — they stay inside ROSE.

Equivalently: L39 = O+S+E = 15+19+5 = 39 (ROSE without R), and R62 = R+O+S+E+E = 18+15+19+5+5 = 62 (ROSE with E doubled). The combo columns are not independent numbers — they all orbit ROSE.
